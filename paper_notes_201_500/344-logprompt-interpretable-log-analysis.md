# 344. LogPrompt: Prompt Engineering Towards Zero-Shot and Interpretable Log Analysis

- **Authors:** Yilun Liu, Shimin Tao, Weibin Meng, Feiyu Yao, Xiaofeng Zhao, Hao Yang, and coauthors
- **Venue / year:** ICSE Companion 2024
- **Paper:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.07610
- **Tags:** `log-analysis` `prompt-engineering` `zero-shot` `interpretability` `anomaly-detection`

## Introduction
Online log analysis has two distinct pain points: traditional parsers and anomaly detectors need in-domain historical logs, and their predictions are often a template or binary label without an explanation. This paper proposes LogPrompt, a prompt-engineering framework that asks an LLM to parse logs, detect anomalies, and justify each decision without in-domain training.

The authors treat interpretation as part of the task rather than a post-hoc visualization. A prompt must constrain the output format, identify variables, explain why a log is normal or abnormal, and remain usable across unfamiliar systems. The paper evaluates whether asking for a justification also improves accuracy by encouraging a more structured reasoning path.

## Method / Framework
LogPrompt has three main prompt strategies. A self-prompt is generated by asking ChatGPT to propose a precise log-analysis instruction and then selecting the best candidate on a validation dataset. A chain-of-thought prompt adds implicit explanation and explicit intermediate rules, with format-control functions to force a label/template output. An in-context prompt supplies a small balanced set of labeled examples and asks the LLM to classify new logs.

The tasks are online log parsing and anomaly detection. For parsing, the model identifies constant tokens and variables and outputs a template. For anomaly detection, the prompt defines normal/abnormal rules and requests a concise reason. The main model is ChatGPT accessed by API; the paper also tests Vicuna-13B. Datasets include HDFS, Hadoop, Zookeeper, BGL, HPC, Linux, Proxifier, Android, and Spirit. The baselines include IPLoM, LogSig, Drain, FT-tree, MoLFI, LKE, LogPPT, LogStamp, DeepLog, LogAnomaly, and other established parsers/detectors.

## Baselines / Comparisons
For log parsing, LogPrompt is compared with classical/heuristic parsers and newer few-shot methods. Most baselines are trained on up to 10% in-domain logs, while LogPrompt uses no in-domain training in the main online setting. For anomaly detection, DeepLog, LogAnomaly, and related forecast/classification systems use extracted templates and training logs; LogPrompt is prompted directly from the log text.

Interpretability is evaluated with six experienced software O&M practitioners from an ICT company. They rate 200 outputs, 100 parsing and 100 anomaly examples, on usefulness and readability using detailed five-point criteria. Incorrect predictions are excluded from this human explanation study so that the analysis focuses on whether correct decisions are understandable.

## Experiments / Findings
On online parsing, LogPrompt achieves the best F1 on six of eight datasets and an average F1 of 0.797. The row includes HDFS 0.863, Hadoop 0.763, Zookeeper 0.889, BGL 0.865, HPC 0.759, Linux 0.766, Proxifier 0.653, and Android 0.819. It outperforms LogPPT by 32.8% and LogStamp by 37.4% in the reported comparison, despite not using in-domain training data.

For anomaly detection, LogPrompt remains competitive without training thousands of logs, but the best session-level F1 is only 0.450. This is an important negative result: a general LLM can explain logs but is not automatically a production-grade anomaly detector because anomaly definitions vary across systems. The authors explicitly say online anomaly detection remains challenging.

The human study yields average usefulness/readability around 4.42/5. Practitioners report that explanations help them understand unfamiliar domains and prepare incident reports quickly. The explanations are most useful when logs contain semantic text; terse codes, digits, addresses, and undocumented parameters remain difficult.

## Ablation / Error Analysis
The prompt ablation is detailed. On BGL, adding implicit CoT raises session F1 from 0.189 to 0.307 and explicit CoT raises it to 0.384; template F1 rises from 0.115 to 0.321. On Spirit, session F1 changes from 0.445 to 0.443/0.450 and template F1 from 0.086 to 0.096/0.116. Thus explanation prompts help most where the task requires logical anomaly reasoning, while simple parsing does not need a long CoT.

The in-context ablation shows that a few labeled examples substantially improve anomaly detection; performance peaks around 20 examples and then decreases as the prompt becomes too long. Prompt candidates also vary: one selected prompt outperforms another by 18.4%, showing that wording and intermediate instructions are part of the algorithm.

Replacing ChatGPT with Vicuna shows that prompt engineering is portable. Vicuna with LogPrompt has comparable parsing performance on HDFS, Linux, and Proxifier and improves its average F1 by 380.7% over its simple-prompt version. This supports local deployment, although performance still varies by dataset.

## Limitations
Public log labels contain errors; the authors identify BGL examples marked abnormal despite normal wording. Baselines may be underestimated because they are trained on only 10% data in the online protocol, while LLMs have large pretraining corpora. The human study has only 200 samples and six practitioners, so significance and evaluator representativeness are limited. API cost, black-box pretraining leakage, prompt sensitivity, and low absolute anomaly F1 limit direct industrial deployment.

## 两句话总结
LogPrompt 用 self-prompt、CoT 和少量 in-context examples，让 ChatGPT/Vicuna 在没有 in-domain 训练的情况下同时做日志解析、异常检测和理由生成。它在解析上取得很强的跨域 F1 和约 4.42/5 的专家可读性，但异常检测绝对性能仍低，说明“能解释日志”不等于“能可靠判断异常”。
